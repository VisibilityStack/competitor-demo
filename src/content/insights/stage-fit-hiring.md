The most counterintuitive lesson in startup hiring: excellence does not transfer across stages by default. An engineer who was genuinely great inside a two-hundred-person scale-up can flounder at a ten-person seed startup, and vice versa, with no drop in talent. When these hires fail, everyone is confused, because the skills were real. What was missing is stage fit.

## What changes between stages

At seed, the job is shipping through ambiguity: no spec, no platform team, no code review culture to lean on, and the roadmap changes weekly. At Series B and beyond, the job is leverage inside a system: coordination, code quality at scale, decisions that survive fifty engineers touching them. Both are hard. They reward opposite instincts. The scale-up engineer waits for structure that never arrives; the seed engineer chafes against structure that exists for good reasons.

## The signals interviews miss

Resumes report companies and titles, not context. "Senior engineer at a unicorn" does not say whether they joined at twenty people or two thousand, owned a system or a slice, thrived on chaos or on craft. Interview stories inherit the same ambiguity, because candidates naturally narrate their proudest, most structured work.

## How to screen for stage explicitly

Ask what scaffolding existed in their best role: who set priorities, who reviewed designs, what happened when requirements were missing. Then describe your reality with uncomfortable honesty and watch the reaction; energized is a signal, politely concerned is a bigger one. Working style assessment helps here too, appetite for ambiguity and autonomy are measurable traits, which is why stage fit is a core dimension in the psychometric screening we run on every shortlist.

## Hire for the stage you are, not the one you are becoming

Founders love hiring ahead, bringing in the Series C engineer at seed "for later." Later has a way of arriving after the mis-hire does.
