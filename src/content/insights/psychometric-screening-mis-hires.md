Most hiring processes measure the wrong thing. A resume shows where someone has been. An interview shows how well they perform in interviews. Neither tells you how a person actually works: how they handle ambiguity, how they respond to feedback, what pace feels natural to them, and what kind of team brings out their best.

That gap is where mis-hires come from. And mis-hires are brutally expensive for software companies: months of lost roadmap, a demoralized team, and a search that starts over from zero.

## What psychometrics actually measure

Psychometric screening is not a personality quiz. Done properly, it profiles the traits that predict on-the-job behavior: conscientiousness, openness, drive, resilience, and working style. Paired with a culture map of your team, it answers a question interviews cannot: will this person do their best work *here*?

Two candidates can have identical resumes and interview equally well. One thrives in your environment and one quietly burns out by month four. The difference is fit, and fit is measurable.

## How we use it in every search

At FPT, every shortlist goes through psychometric testing and culture mapping before you meet a single candidate. We include it at no extra cost because we consider it part of doing the job properly, not an upsell.

The screen works in both directions. It protects you from a hire who interviews well but works differently than your team needs. And it protects candidates from landing in an environment where they cannot succeed. Both failure modes end the same way: an empty seat and a restarted search.

## What founders see

The results show up in the numbers we track across engagements: retention improves when fit is screened, and team satisfaction rises when new hires integrate quickly instead of clashing with the people around them.

The pattern is consistent. Companies that treat hiring like product, measured and iterated, out-hire companies that rely on gut feel. Psychometrics are simply the instrumentation.
